Pythagoras' Theorem

The oldest surviving relationship in geometry: in any right-angled triangle, the square built on the hypotenuse always equals the sum of the squares built on the other two sides.

a² + b² = c²

What the theorem says

Take any right triangle — one with a 90° corner — and call the two shorter sides (the legs) a and b, and the longest side (opposite the right angle, the hypotenuse) c. The theorem states that the square of the hypotenuse always equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

It's not just an equation to memorize — it's a statement about area. If you build an actual square on each of the three sides of the triangle, the areas of the two smaller squares always add up exactly to the area of the square on the hypotenuse, no matter the triangle's proportions, as long as the angle is a right angle.

Use the tabs above to explore where this comes from: History traces it back nearly 4,000 years, Proof shows two different ways to see why it's true, Why It Matters covers where it shows up today, and Interactive Lab lets you drag a live triangle and watch the relationship hold.

A theorem older than its name

Despite carrying his name, Pythagoras almost certainly did not discover this relationship — and there is no surviving proof that he ever wrote one down himself. Historians note that the theorem was credited to him largely because his followers at Croton attributed all new results to their founder under strict vows of secrecy.

c. 1900–1800 BCE
Babylon & Egypt

The relationship appears on the clay tablet Plimpton 322, roughly 4,000 years old — over a thousand years before Pythagoras was born.

c. 800–600 BCE
India — the Śulba Sūtras

The Baudhāyana and Apastamba Śulba Sūtras describe the rule using a rope, for the purpose of constructing perfectly square fire altars.

c. 570–495 BCE
Pythagoras of Samos

Studied under Thales and Anaximander, travelled to Egypt and Babylon, then founded a school in Croton — where the theorem was formally attached to his name.

1st century BCE
China — Nine Chapters

The Jiuzhang Suanshu (Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art) records an elegant "gestalt" proof, rearranging squares purely by sight — no algebra required.

c. 300 BCE
Euclid's Elements

Formalised as Proposition 47 of Book I — the rigorous, axiomatic proof that became the standard reference for over two thousand years.

Today
The World's Most-Proved Theorem

Guinness World Records recognises it as the most-proved theorem in mathematics, with contributors ranging from Leonardo da Vinci to a young Albert Einstein to U.S. President James Garfield.

Why it still matters

// 01

Distance & Navigation

GPS, mapping, and every straight-line distance calculation between two points rests on this exact relationship.

// 02

Construction & Engineering

Builders use the 3-4-5 rule — a direct application — to square corners and verify right angles on site without any instruments.

// 03

Physics

Extended to more dimensions, it underlies the energy–momentum relation in particle physics and the field equations of relativity.

// 04

Computer Graphics

Every distance, collision check, and vector length in 2D/3D graphics and game engines is a Pythagorean calculation.

// 05

Trigonometry's Foundation

The identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 — used throughout MATH.LAB's own Trigonometry page — is the theorem in disguise on a unit circle.

// 06

Everyday Estimation

From sizing a TV screen to checking if a ladder safely reaches a wall, it turns a spatial question into simple arithmetic.
