Triangles

A three-sided polygon that forms the basis of all geometry. Explore its profound history, how to classify its shapes, and interact with it.

Formulation & History

A triangle is formulated by three non-collinear points connected by three line segments. The sum of its interior angles is always 180 degrees in Euclidean geometry. Its fundamental area calculation is: Area = (1/2) × base × height.

Past: Ancient Egyptians utilized triangles (specifically the 3-4-5 right triangle) for land surveying and constructing the pyramids, long before formal theorems existed. The Greeks, including Pythagoras and Euclid, established rigorous mathematical proofs defining their properties.

Present: Today, triangles are the structural backbone of engineering and architecture (seen in bridge trusses and geodesic domes). In computer graphics, every complex 3D shape is rendered using millions of tiny triangles, as three points are guaranteed to always lie on a single flat plane.

Future: Triangles will continue to be essential in fields like topology, space exploration (triangulation for celestial navigation), and advanced molecular modeling (like the geometric structures of new materials).


Equilateral Triangle

A triangle in which all three sides have the same length. Because the sides are equal, all three internal angles are also equal, each measuring exactly 60°.

Proof of Angles

Let the sides be a = b = c. By the Law of Sines, a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) = c/sin(C). Since a = b = c, then sin(A) = sin(B) = sin(C). For angles in a triangle, this implies A = B = C. Since A + B + C = 180°, each must be 60°.

Basic Problem

Find the perimeter of an equilateral triangle with a side length of 5 cm.

Solution: The perimeter is the sum of all sides. Since all 3 sides are equal:
Perimeter = 3 × 5 cm = 15 cm.

Complex Problem

Derive the area of an equilateral triangle with side length s, and find the area when s = 10.

Solution: Drawing an altitude splits the triangle into two 30-60-90 right triangles. The base is s/2 and the hypotenuse is s. By Pythagoras, height h = √(s² - (s/2)²) = √(3s²/4) = s√3 / 2.

Area = (1/2) × base × height = (1/2) × s × (s√3 / 2) = (s²√3) / 4.

For s = 10: Area = (100√3) / 4 = 25√3 ≈ 43.3 sq units.



Isosceles Triangle

A triangle that has at least two sides of equal length. The angles opposite the equal sides are also equal, known as the base angles.

Proof of Base Angles (Isosceles Theorem)

Consider triangle ABC with AB = AC. Draw the angle bisector of ∠A, intersecting BC at D. Then in triangles ABD and ACD: AB = AC (given), ∠BAD = ∠CAD (by bisector), and AD = AD (common). By SAS congruence, ΔABD ≅ ΔACD. Therefore, ∠B = ∠C.

Basic Problem

In an isosceles triangle, the two equal sides are 8 cm each, and the base is 6 cm. What is the perimeter?

Solution: Perimeter = side1 + side2 + base
= 8 + 8 + 6 = 22 cm.

Complex Problem

An isosceles triangle has two equal sides of length 13 cm and a base of 10 cm. Find its area.

Solution: Draw an altitude to the base. It bisects the base into two 5 cm segments, forming a right triangle with hypotenuse 13 and leg 5.

Using Pythagoras: h² + 5² = 13² ➔ h² + 25 = 169 ➔ h² = 144 ➔ h = 12 cm.

Area = (1/2) × base × height = (1/2) × 10 × 12 = 60 sq cm.

Scalene Triangle

A triangle in which all three sides have different lengths, and consequently, all three internal angles have different measures.

Proof of Triangle Inequality Theorem

For any triangle with sides a, b, and c, it must hold true that a + b > c, a + c > b, and b + c > a. The shortest distance between two points is a straight line. Thus, the length of one side must strictly be less than the combined lengths of the other two sides.

Basic Problem

Can a triangle be formed with side lengths 3 cm, 4 cm, and 8 cm?

Solution: We check the Triangle Inequality Theorem. Is 3 + 4 > 8? No, 7 is not greater than 8. Therefore, these sides cannot form a valid triangle.

Complex Problem

Find the area of a scalene triangle with side lengths 7 cm, 24 cm, and 25 cm using Heron's formula.

Solution: First, calculate the semi-perimeter s = (7 + 24 + 25) / 2 = 56 / 2 = 28.

Heron's Formula: Area = √(s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c))
= √(28 × (28-7) × (28-24) × (28-25))
= √(28 × 21 × 4 × 3)
= √(7056) = 84 sq cm.

(Note: Since 7² + 24² = 25², it is also a right triangle!)
