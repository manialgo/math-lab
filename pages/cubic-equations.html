Cubic Equations

One degree higher again — an S-shaped curve that can cross the x-axis up to three times. This is also where factorization stopped being enough, and mathematicians had to go looking for a general formula.

The idea

A cubic equation in one variable has the general form:

ax³ + bx² + cx + d = 0 (a ≠ 0)

Its graph is an S-shaped curve with up to two turning points, so it can cross the x-axis once, twice (with a repeated root), or three times — meaning a cubic has up to three real roots.

Unlike linear or quadratic equations, most cubics don't factor neatly by inspection. The most practical approach in a classroom is the Rational Root Theorem: try small integer divisors of the constant term d as candidate roots, confirm one by substitution, then divide it out (see the Polynomials page for synthetic division) to reduce the cubic to a quadratic.

A true general formula does exist — Cardano's formula — but it's considerably more involved than the quadratic formula, which is part of why it took mathematicians until the 16th century to find it.

Worked example

x³ − 6x² + 11x − 6 = 0

Method 1 — Factorization (via trial root + synthetic division)

By the Rational Root Theorem, try divisors of −6: ±1, ±2, ±3, ±6. Testing x = 1:

(1)³ − 6(1)² + 11(1) − 6 = 1 − 6 + 11 − 6 = 0 ✓

So (x − 1) is a factor. Dividing the cubic by (x − 1) using synthetic division gives a quotient of x² − 5x + 6, so:

x³ − 6x² + 11x − 6 = (x − 1)(x² − 5x + 6) = (x − 1)(x − 2)(x − 3)

Setting each factor to zero: x = 1, 2, 3.

Method 2 — Using Cardano's formula

First, depress the cubic by substituting x = t − b/3a, which removes the x² term. For ax³+bx²+cx+d, this gives an equation of the form t³ + pt + q = 0, where:

p = (3ac − b²) / 3a² q = (2b³ − 9abc + 27a²d) / 27a³

The real root of the depressed cubic is then given by Cardano's formula:

t = ∛(−q/2 + √(q²/4 + p³/27)) + ∛(−q/2 − √(q²/4 + p³/27))

Substituting a = 1, b = −6, c = 11, d = −6 through this formula produces t = 0, and reversing the substitution x = t − b/3a = t + 2 gives x = 2 — one of the same three roots found by factorization, confirming the two methods agree. In practice, once one root is found, factorization is almost always the faster route for a cubic with rational roots; Cardano's formula matters most when no rational root exists at all.
