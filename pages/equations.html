Equations & Roots

Before linear, quadratic, or cubic equations, there's one shared idea underneath all of them: finding the value that makes a statement true. This page is the starting point — pick a branch below once you've got the idea.

What is an equation?

An equation is a mathematical statement that says two expressions are equal, joined by an "=" sign — for example, 2x + 3 = 11. It's a claim, not yet a fact: it's only true for certain values of the unknown (here, x).

What is a root?

A root (or solution) of an equation is any value of the unknown that makes the statement true when substituted in. For 2x + 3 = 11, substituting x = 4 gives 2(4) + 3 = 11, which checks out — so x = 4 is a root.

An equation can have one root, several roots, infinitely many, or none at all, depending on its shape. A big part of algebra is figuring out how many roots to expect and how to find them systematically, rather than by guessing.

Worked example

3x − 7 = 2

Add 7 to both sides: 3x = 9. Divide both sides by 3: x = 3. Check: 3(3) − 7 = 2 ✓ — so x = 3 is the root.

A short history

Equation-solving is one of the oldest branches of mathematics. Babylonian mathematicians (around 2000 BCE) were already solving what we'd now call quadratic equations using geometric methods, though they had no algebraic notation for it. Ancient Egyptian and Chinese texts show similar problems solved by trial methods and proportional reasoning.

The word "algebra" itself comes from al-jabr, a term used by the Persian mathematician Al-Khwarizmi in the 9th century, describing the process of moving a term from one side of an equation to the other. His systematic treatment of linear and quadratic equations laid the groundwork for algebra as a formal discipline.

Cubic equations resisted a general algebraic solution for far longer — it wasn't until the 16th century, through the work of Italian mathematicians including Scipione del Ferro, Niccolò Tartaglia, and Gerolamo Cardano, that a general formula for cubic roots was published.

Why it matters

Equations are how quantitative relationships get expressed and solved — from working out how long a journey takes, to modelling projectile motion, to balancing a budget, to designing the curves an engineer needs for a bridge. Every one of those problems eventually reduces to: here's a relationship, now find the unknown that satisfies it. That's exactly what solving an equation does.
